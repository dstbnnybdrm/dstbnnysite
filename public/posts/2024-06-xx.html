web graphics 101

xx, may xx, 2024

one of the things that i loved about the older web and still love in the current personal/indie/whatever web space is the culture around sharing graphics! wacky GIFs, tacky text art, ... . often webmasters create pages dedicated to sharing their own personal collection they've accrued over time. and despite the older internet being much more of a wild west type place there still arised some standards around graphics, and i think that that's super fascinating! so this'll be a little introduction and explanation behind some of the more popular web graphic formats there are!

88×31

probably the most common type of shared web graphic you'll find are images of 88×31 dimensions. despite both their abundance and prevalence there doesn't seem to be an official or collectively agreed upon name for them, though they commonly go by variations of either "buttons", "badges", or "banners". there was a time when the IAB called ads of these dimensions "Micro Bars" which is what i've stuck with, at least for naming the folder i keep them in for this site. though, you'll mostly see people just referring to them as buttons!

you'd be right in thinking this width to height ratio seems a bit arbitrary. normally you'd expect to see simple, round numbers, especially in a standard! the history behind the 88×31 is pretty interesting, going back to the mid-90s, and involves both Netscape and GeoCities to an extent. the mid-90s was the start of the first Browser War, Netscape Navigator was the most used browser then but Internet Explorer had just come out and, unlike Netscape, was free and came bundled with their operating systems. this newfound competition resulted in each of them introducing proprietary HTML that could only be seen on their respective browsers, probably most infamously, the <blink> tag in Netscape and, a little more beloved, the <marquee> tag in Internet Explorer. sites that utilised Netscape-specific features were encouraged to join the Netscape Now Program, offering to display their logos for free in exchange for displaying the classic "Netscape Now!" button prominently on their site.

on how the format gained traction from here, Neonaut says perhaps the combination of bragging rights and aesthetic helped this button style gain popularity.

on GeoCities, the once massive web hosting service, it was required for free pages to link to back to the GeoCities page. as far as i'm aware, they didn't provide banners/buttons to link back to, so i can only assume others, or even GeoCities themselves, matched the size and style of the Netscape Now! button if it was already popular. i can also imagine that there were those that even didn't apply to the Netscape Now Program and were using the button on their sites. webmasters at the time would let users know which browser was best for viewing their site to the fullest which spawned even more button variants, like the ones promoting Internet Explorer.

what's interesting too is it isn't necessarily just the dimensions of these buttons that define them, at least traditionally. there's a kind of "standard" to the classic 88×31 button look, namely the beveled edges and the gray background, some even keep to the "Now!" theme, taking the same text from the original Netscape button, though of course, many buttons branch away from that, especially more modern ones.

88×31 buttons since the Netscape times range from promotion of one's own, or others' sites, expression of one's interests or identity, copyright notices and licenses, to just silly little goofs and gags. you'll often find personal sites linking to other personal sites with a "button wall", large collections of these style of buttons linking to their creators, and it's how i imagine a lot of these buttons get shared and passed around.
