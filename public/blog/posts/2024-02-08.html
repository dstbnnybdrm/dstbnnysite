i got my very first electric guitar sometime in, i think, 2017 on my birthday: a cheap, black and white Squier Stratocaster (the only left-handed model in our price-range they had at the time) with a used Line VI Spider amp, i still have both to this day! since then i've accumulated a not-insignificant amount of instruments, toys, and other oddities i've managed to get my hands on (maybe i'll write about all of those sometime). but the topic for this particular post are my electric guitars! i've gotten two more since then: my trusty Fender Telecaster, tentatively named "Sunny," and my recent love, my Squire Classic Vibe Mustang "Langley."

i've gotten a lot of use out of those two instruments in the past few years, but every once in a while i feel the weight of my neglect for that very first electric guitar. admittedly the electronics on it can leave a little (or a lot) to be desired and so i never felt the need to use it for recording since the other two guitars suited my purposes a little bit better. but since i had it i felt like i needed to at least try and find a purpose for it; maybe give it some sort of a niche that i don't currently have filled.

experiment 01: Ostrich tuning

the first of my experiments with my Squire Strat was trying out some tuning shenanigans. i had just gotten into Sonic Youth at the time and figured i had a safe candidate to try out their tunings on. the one i stuck with for a while was the tuning for my fav SY song, Schizophrenia: 

F#F#GGAA
. it just has this strange, dissonant, but ethereal quality to it that i loved. of course, you can't just tune a regular set of guitar strings to this--well i guess you can but you'd lose like half of your strings--so i opted to use the closest gauge counterparts that i had lying around: 2 low E's, 2 A's, and 2 G's (note that i had to file down the nut slots to do this, so proceed with caution if you want to try it; you can't undo filing!). i left Ramiel like this for a bit and even recorded a DREAM DISPHORIA song with it (tunings like this are great for stuff like noise, drone, ambient, etc.!).

one cool unintended thing about the Schizophrenia tuning is that it's very close to being an "Ostrich tuning," or a "trivial tuning," one where all the strings are the same note. as much as i liked the dreamy vibe of the Schizophrenia tuning i found i that i couldn't find many uses for it, and would end up leaving Ramiel untouched for extended periods of timd again. eventually after fiddling around with the strings for a bit i just left it in a trivial G tuning (i.e GGGggg, lmao). as silly as it might look, giving myself less notes right off the bat made this guitar more useful to me somehow. i like the way Yvette Young describes guitar tunings, that they each kind of "colour" the sound in their own way, so in the case of Ostrich tuning i think of it as like a monochrome palette. i used it to add these super jangly and drone-y overdubs to some songs by capo-ing at whatever would get me at the song's key and just fretting a couple strings at the root notes and letting the rest ring out. if we're still using this colour analogy i guess it'd be like applying a colour filter on top of the song or a blending mode.

now, you may have guessed, but Ostrich tuning can be a bit limiting to say the least lol. while it was fun for a bit to try and work within the boundaries of this tuning, there's only so much you can do, and more importantly i felt like it was making Ramiel a guitar purely for overdubs, and never just playing her on her own. which can't be right! Ramiel is her own guitar! i just needed to find another purpose for her.

experiment 02: basitar

enter experiment number two! one day i put on a youtube video by david hilowitz (creator of decentsampler!) where he finds and restores a super decrepit guitar and turns it into a "basitar." he goes into it in the video, but basically, a regular six string electric guitar but with two bass strings instead, used by one of the guys in the Presidents of the United States of America. i loved how it sounded and the guitar in the video being a black strat-style guitar gave me an idea. so i stripped all the strings (insert stripping off G-string joke) off of Ramiel and took the 2 highest strings off of my bass guitar (the d and g strings). the basitar is a very niche concept, so there isn't really a lot of information on it or how to set one up, no where does it say to use the D and G strings of a bass, but i approximated it with the closest that i had to what i think are the gauges that Chris of POTUS uses.

(since we're talking about information online, i did see a lot of people saying not to do this lol. that it would destroy the neck of the guitar, warp it from the tension, etc. ... but i'll be honest i do think that people tend to overreact a little when it comes to guitar setup, adjustments, and modifications. modern guitars are a lot more resilient than some might think! now, i still wouldn't recommend trying much of what i'm doing here, especially if it's your first time doing any of these adjustments, but if you think you know what you're doing and have some knowledge under your belt then i say go right ahead!)

pretty much every instance i've seen of chris's basitar or other people's imitations were the bass strings being in the A and G string positions of the guitar. my guess is it's counteracts the larger thickness of the strings so they wouldn't be too close together and more like a bass guitars string distance. after just slapping the strings on i was surprised at how well Ramiel took the much higher gauges. they were pretty much playable immediately, to a certain extent , and with some simple adjustments she was a pretty decent playing guitar! or, uh, basitar! the filed-down nut slots i had already done for the SY tuning were actually already pretty close to what i needed for these new strings to fit snug. the way bass strings feel on a standard six string guitar is also surprisingly different. the shorter scale-length makes frets easier to reach of course, but there's a kind of "thumpiness" (?) there that i love and kind of reminds me of, say, a fender mustang bass. the tuning i went with was DA (same as POTUS but they tuned a half-step lower) so it actually occupies the same range as a normal guitar in drop D, but the bigger string gauges give the sound so much more weight and heaviness, it feels lower even though i know it's technically not.

Ramiel

i loved how it felt and sounded and how it was just limiting enough to feel creative in without being too stifling. one of the things i like about bass strings is that, to me, it feels a lot easier to tap on them. i guess something about the larger gauges makes them easier to ring out when tapped, and this applies to the basitar as well! but tapping got me thinking about twinkly emo and math rock (well, i guess i'm always thinking about twinkly emo), and that gave me another idea. what if i add just one more string? a high one, so i could get an interesting juxtaposition between the meaty low notes and also some twinkly high stuff. so i took an extra high E string i had and strung it in the B position. i tried it in the actual high E position first, and it definitely worked, but i actually preferred the closer proximity to the A string. since the D and A strings are now one whole string apart, if the E and A strings are closer you can actually fret both of them with a barre. and since E and A are a fifth apart you get a power chord really easily without touching the D string (and of course, D and A also make a power chord, so there's two ways to get power chords here!). i left the high E string as an E note so now the tuning is DAe, coincidentally half of the famous American Football tuning: DAEac#e. i love emo. so in effect i basically took the already niche idea of a basitar and altered it even more to fit my personal needs. i'm pretty sure i'm the only person to have tried this particular setup, at least from looking around online.

i've always operated under the assumption that when i buy a guitar, or any instrument, it's mine to keep forever. i've always found it kind of stupid that as soon as you make the tiniest change to your instrument the resale value plummets to oblivion. i think you should make your instruments unabashedly yours! mod them, do whatever is gonna connect you to your instrument more and make it easier to express yourself on them. it's why i cover my instruments in stickers and give them silly names and do all sorts of stuff to them.
