RPG Maker

RPG Maker games are something i only really got into around the height of the COVID-19 pandemic but have very quickly fallen in love with. among my very first was "Yume Nikki" and that was a special experience that i feel like you can only really have once; just the atmosphere, the freedom and the aimlessness, it all culminated into such a unique sense of comfort and escapism that i think i really needed at the time.

i got into some of the classics shortly after: games like "OFF", "ib", "Re:Kinder", etc., all with their own unique blends of horror, the surreal, and a tiny bit of tongue-in-cheek self awareness, yet with a paradoxically sobering earnestness. there's a kind of culture you can see when you play a number of RPG Maker games that i just that i just fell in love with so immediately; they can feel so intimate and personal—almost to the point of feeling uncomfortably close sometimes—and that's not something you necessarily get a lot anywhere else.

i felt like sharing a few of my favourites and some words to go along with them!

Yume Nikki

it's truly difficult to put into words anything that hasn't been said before about this game. i think the easiest and only complete way to get a sense of it is to simply experience it for yourself.

Yume Nikki seemed to have just popped suddenly into existence in 2004, its last official update happening about 3 years later, and with little heard from the game's developer, KIKIYAMA, since. and i think the mysteries surrounding Yume Nikki's creator, coupled with its atmosphere and the subject matter itself, adds to the allure and mystique of the game for a lot of people; by its very nature there are open ends everywhere in almost every aspect of this game and it's so fascinating to dig through.

at first glance it's probably unclear why this game has had such an impact on people. when i tell my uninitiated friends about Yume Nikki i tell them that it's a game about walking around and exploring a dream world, and that's it. i make sure to explicitly say that there is no "goal" in the traditional sense, save for collecting all the "effects" and subsequently achieving the ending, which are both entirely optional. but, to me, that's why Yume Nikki is so special and why it's achieved the devoted following that it has. there's a particularly unique blend of emotions in the game's aimlessness: some parts comforting, some uneasy; some parts familiar, some totally alien. there's so much that's unexplained but that you know must mean something, right? like you've been dropped into somebody's subconscious (and, in a way, you have).

that open-endedness and lack of direction lends itself to being a deeply personal experience for each person that plays it, and is why i personally would recommend playing it completely blind, at least for the first time. people discover new areas in different orders, find things in different ways, interpret things differently. it's a joy watching somebody play this game and seeing how much their experience differed from yours. it almost acts like a Rorschach test in a way: what you take away from it is individual to your subjective experiences; different things will imprint more onto different people. there's a number of fan theories regarding what exactly is happening and what is trying to be said, if anything, sprouting from recurring bits of imagery and general themes that people have picked up on.

to me, Yume Nikki embodies a strange kind of comfort that's hard to articulate, maybe because i played it during a particularly vulnerable time. something about having an entire world to explore at my own pace, nothing expected of me, . there were a lot of moments that caught me off guard with their emotional resonance.

there's a traincar you can find that takes you from one area to another.

i feel like Yume Nikki gets misconstrued as a horror game often. it certainly isn't the most optimistic game, at least at a surface level. there's a heavy weight of isolation as you explore this world on your own, areas like the Number World that feel almost hostile, and moments that can be genuinely unsettling, but to label it "horror" feels like a huge undercut, in my opinion. Yume Nikki can be scary in the same way it can be scary to be alone with your own thoughts; the frightening aspects feel less like The Point and more like a natural outcome to the surreal, free associative nature of the game.
